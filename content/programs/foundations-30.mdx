## What This Program Is

Foundations of Kettlebell Mastery is for athletes who know the movements and are ready to build something durable with them. Whether you've completed My First Program or you're returning to kettlebell training to rebuild your technique from the ground up, this is where the real work begins. Four weeks, four sessions per week, across the swing, clean, and snatch — progressing in volume, density, and demand each week until the final session brings everything together.

---

## The Philosophy Behind It

The goal of this program is ownership. Not exposure — ownership. You can be introduced to a movement in a weekend. Owning it under accumulated fatigue, at higher volume, with consistent quality across every rep in every session — that takes longer. Foundations is built to close that gap.

The program starts with an anti-glycolytic approach in Week 1: generous rest, short work intervals, full attention on quality. This is intentional. Technique established at speed-of-practice transfers to speed-of-performance. As weeks progress, the rest periods tighten, the work intervals extend, and the total volume increases — not to test your conditioning, but to find out whether the technique you built in Week 1 survives fatigue. It should. If it doesn't, the program tells you clearly: velocity drops, consistency scores fall, and the data shows you exactly where the pattern broke.

Progression in this program is gated by velocity retention. The app measures your average velocity in the first third of each block and compares it to the last third. If you're holding 70% or more of your opening pace through the end of the block, you advance. If you're not, the session repeats. That threshold exists because technique degradation and power loss travel together. Falling below 70% retention isn't just a conditioning failure — it means the quality of every rep in the back half of your session is compromised.

Each week is anchored by a dedicated session for each movement — a swing day, a clean day, a snatch day — plus a combination session that links all three. This structure ensures focused practice per movement while also developing the capacity to transition between them. The combination sessions get harder each week: shorter rest, more total rounds, higher demand on your ability to sustain quality across different patterns in sequence.

## Week by Week

**Week 1 — Patterning & Tempo**

The foundation week. Swing and clean sessions run anti-glycolytic: work intervals of 30 seconds with generous rest. The swing day is 6 rounds. The clean day is 12 rounds per arm, alternating. Every rep is evaluated on quality, not quantity. The combination session on Day 4 runs clean first, then swing as a finisher — linking the patterns before the week is out. The only standard this week is that every rep earns its place.

**Week 2 — Building Endurance**

The snatch arrives in Week 2. It's introduced after a swing primer — the same hinge pattern, now redirected overhead. Work intervals extend to 35–40 seconds across all movements. Rest tightens. The clean day moves to equal work-to-rest. The mixed session on Day 4 cuts clean rest to 25 seconds — the first real accumulation pressure of the program. You'll notice the difference. The goal is to absorb it without the technique breaking.

**Week 3 — Power & Precision**

Week 3 asks for output. The swing day becomes 10 rounds at 40 seconds of work with 40 seconds of rest — equal work-to-rest across a high-round block. The clean day reaches 16 rounds, also at 40/20. This is density work: maximum clean volume under short rest, maintaining rack quality in rounds 14 and 15 the same as in rounds 1 and 2. The snatch day runs 12 rounds with 35 seconds of work. The combination session introduces swing, clean, and snatch in sequence under progressive fatigue. Velocity retention is now the primary signal — you should see from your data exactly where the pattern holds and where it cracks.

**Week 4 — Mastery**

The final week removes the buffer. Rest periods are at their tightest. Volume is at its peak. The Grand Finale — the last session — runs swing, clean, and snatch back-to-back, with nearly no rest in the clean block and only 15 seconds between intervals. The RKC Plank in the warmup is held for 30 seconds — you'll remember when it was held for 20. The cool-down ends the same way the first session began: breathing on the floor, acknowledging the work. The difference between those two moments is the program.

## What Success Looks Like

By the end of this program:

- Velocity retention holds above 70% in swing and clean blocks — your pace in round 10 is recognizably close to round 1
- The snatch lockout is consistent — arm straight, shoulder packed, no soft elbow — across all rounds, not just the first two
- The clean rack is quiet across high-volume blocks, including in the density days of Week 3 when fatigue is highest
- The combination sessions feel manageable — moving between swing, clean, and snatch in sequence no longer requires mental recalibration between movements
- You've trained four sessions per week for four consecutive weeks — you understand what consistent kettlebell training feels like in your body
