## What This Program Is

Iron Continuum is a strength-and-ballistics integration program for athletes who want to develop both qualities simultaneously without sacrificing either. Three sessions per week, each pairing a ballistic block with a grind block and anchored by the Turkish get-up. Eight weeks of structured progression across the overhead press, goblet squat, one-arm row, swing, snatch, and clean. If your ballistic technique is sound and you've never trained genuine kettlebell strength systematically, this is the program that closes that gap.

---

## The Philosophy Behind It

Ballistic and strength training are often treated as opposing training modes — you program one or the other, and you periodize between them. Iron Continuum rejects that framing. The two qualities are not in conflict. They are complementary, and training them together produces an athlete who is both powerful and structurally durable.

The structure of each session makes this explicit. Each day opens with a ballistic block — swing, clean, or snatch — trained anti-glycolytically with full rest. Power comes first, while the nervous system is fresh. The ballistic block is followed by a strength block — overhead press, goblet squat, or one-arm row — trained with long work intervals and deliberate tempo. This sequencing matters: the ballistic establishes hip drive and power output, and the grind block that follows asks the body to produce controlled, loaded movement under accumulated fatigue. That is exactly the demand of real-world athletic performance.

The Turkish get-up appears in every single session as the third block. This is not optional and it is not accessory work. The get-up is the most effective diagnostic tool in kettlebell training — it exposes instability, asymmetry, and compensation that shorter movements can hide. Training it weekly alongside ballistics and grinds ensures that structural integrity is monitored throughout the program, not just at the beginning.

Progression across 8 weeks moves through four phases: Control Establishment (Weeks 1–2), Rep Progression (Weeks 3–4), Round Expansion (Weeks 5–6), and Density Control (Weeks 7–8). Each phase adds a new layer of demand — first establishing movement quality, then increasing reps, then adding rounds, then compressing rest. By Week 8, the performance sessions ask for higher ballistic round counts and maximum output in the grind blocks. The Turkish get-up standard never changes: one rep per side, slow and controlled. If that's getting worse, something is overloading the system.

Velocity retention is tracked on the ballistic blocks and must hold above 70% to advance. Consistency on the grind blocks is tracked separately — the primary metric there is rep pace, not peak velocity. These are genuinely different movement qualities requiring different data to assess.

## Week by Week

**Weeks 1–2 — Control Establishment**

Three sessions: swing and press on Day 1, clean and squat on Day 2, snatch and row on Day 3. Every session closes with the Turkish get-up. The ballistic standard is anti-glycolytic — generous rest, full power every rep. Strength block targets are moderate: 5 reps per side on the press, 8 on the squat, 8 on the row. The goal of these two weeks is not accumulation — it is establishing the quality standard for every movement that will be loaded progressively from Week 3 forward.

**Weeks 3–4 — Rep Progression**

The strength block reps increase: press adds one rep per side (5 → 6), squat and row each add two (8 → 10). Ballistic volume and structure stay consistent from the control phase. The progression is deliberate and modest — this is not a block where you feel buried, it's the block where the new baseline is set. By the end of Week 4, you're doing meaningful strength work at higher reps than you started with, while maintaining the same ballistic quality.

**Weeks 5–6 — Round Expansion**

Strength blocks add a round and rest compresses slightly. The grind volume increases without the rep target changing — more total sets at the same rep count. The ballistic blocks hold at 6 rounds for swing, 12 for clean and snatch. The combination of more grind volume and slightly less rest begins to test recovery between blocks. This is where the program starts to feel harder. The Turkish get-up remains unchanged — one rep per side per session, for the 12th week in a row.

**Weeks 7–8 — Density Control and Performance**

Rest compresses further across both ballistic and grind blocks. The same reps, same rounds — produced faster, with less buffer between sets. Week 8 is the performance week: ballistic round counts increase by two on every day, and the grind blocks call for maximum quality reps — full range on the press, deepest squat, full scapular retraction on the row. The final session of the program tests the full spectrum: snatch, one-arm row, and Turkish get-up at peak demand. This is what eight weeks of consistent integration has built.

## What Success Looks Like

By the end of this program:

- You can overhead press and complete a goblet squat at meaningful loads relative to your starting point — the strength work has accumulated into something real
- Velocity retention on ballistic blocks holds above 70% through the density weeks — fatigue has increased but power output has not degraded proportionally
- The Turkish get-up is measurably cleaner than Week 1 — transitions are more deliberate, compensation has decreased
- The combination of a ballistic block followed immediately by a grind block feels manageable — the ability to transition between power and control within a session is now trained
- Three sessions per week has been consistent for 8 weeks — the training frequency is embedded as a habit, not just completed as a program
