## What This Program Is

Power Cycle is an 8-week training block for athletes who want to move the bell faster. Not for longer. Not with more reps. Faster — with maximum force expression in every rep, and enough recovery between sets to reproduce it. This is alactic power training: short, explosive bursts with full rest, repeated across a progressively demanding structure. If you train three days a week and want peak bell speed to be your primary adaptation, this is the program.

---

## The Philosophy Behind It

There is a meaningful difference between conditioning and power. Most kettlebell programs train conditioning — the ability to sustain output over time. Conditioning is important, but it is not the same as power, and training one does not automatically develop the other. Power Cycle exists specifically to develop the latter.

Alactic power training works on a simple principle: the phosphocreatine energy system — the one responsible for maximum-effort explosive output — depletes in roughly 8–10 seconds of all-out work and replenishes in approximately 60–90 seconds of rest. When you train within these windows, every set is an opportunity for full power expression. When you violate them — when you shorten the rest to save time or push through fatigue — you stop training power and start training lactic endurance. This program never makes that trade.

The first four weeks use 20-second work intervals with 60–100 seconds of rest. Five reps per arm on the snatch and clean. Ten reps per swing. The bell should be moving as fast as possible on every single rep. Not "pretty fast." Maximum intent. The velocity data the app collects during these sessions is the signal: if average velocity drops meaningfully from round 1 to round 8, the rest wasn't long enough.

Weeks 3 and 4 increase round counts — not by shortening rest, but by adding rounds. The total session volume goes up while the per-set quality standard stays the same. This is accumulation at the alactic level: more total power output without compromising the output per set.

Weeks 5 and 6 extend the work intervals from 20 to 30 seconds and increase target reps. The rest periods remain generous. The nervous system now has to sustain maximum intent for a slightly longer window before recovery, which requires a different kind of focus — not more effort, but more sustained focus within the same effort.

Weeks 7 and 8 compress rest slightly and ask you to hold the same power output you've been building. The final session of Week 8 is a benchmark: 20 snatch rounds at 30 seconds of work with 60 seconds of rest. What your velocity curve looks like across those 20 rounds is the honest result of the program.

Progression is gated at 70% velocity retention — the highest standard of any program in the library. This program has no interest in volume accomplishments. It cares about one thing: how fast the bell is moving.

## Week by Week

**Weeks 1–2 — Neural Familiarization**

Three sessions: a snatch and clean power day, a swing and snatch power day, and a mixed power day. Every session is built around 20-second work intervals with 70–100 seconds of rest. Five reps per arm. Ten swings. The movement variety across days ensures all three ballistics are trained at maximum intent, not just the one you're best at. The neural familiarization label is accurate — the first two weeks establish what maximum power output actually feels like in each movement, which is the baseline everything else is built on.

**Weeks 3–4 — Volume Addition**

Round counts increase: snatch goes from 16 to 20 rounds, cleans from 12 to 20, swings from 8 to 10. Rest tightens modestly to 60 seconds. The work interval stays at 20 seconds. More sets of maximum-intent reps, same standard per set. If your velocity held across 16 snatch rounds in Week 1, you now find out if it holds across 20.

**Weeks 5–6 — Work Extension**

Work intervals extend to 30 seconds. Rep targets increase to 8 per arm on snatches and cleans, 15 on swings. Rest remains generous at 60–100 seconds. The 30-second interval is long enough that it demands a mid-set decision: do you hold the throttle fully open or pace yourself? Power Cycle's answer is always the former. If you can't sustain full power for 30 seconds with full recovery, the weight is too heavy.

**Weeks 7–8 — Power Retention Stress**

Rest compresses slightly. The question the program is now asking is direct: can you produce the same power output you built in Weeks 5 and 6 with a little less recovery? Week 8's final session — 20 snatch rounds, 30 seconds of work, 60 seconds of rest — is the benchmark. Your velocity data across all 20 rounds is the result. There is no ambiguity about whether the program worked.

---

## What Success Looks Like

By the end of this program:

- Your average peak velocity on swing and snatch rounds in the benchmark session matches or exceeds your Week 1 baseline — you're producing the same power at higher volume
- Velocity retention across the 20-round benchmark holds above 70% — your last rounds are not significantly slower than your first
- The 30-second work intervals feel sustainable at maximum intent rather than requiring pacing — the alactic system has expanded
- The mixed sessions feel coordinated — transitioning between movements doesn't require a reset period
