## What This Program Is

One Bell Standard is a 12-week program built around a single movement and a single goal. The movement is the one-arm swing. The goal is The Standard: 10 reps right arm, 10 reps left arm, every minute for 5 minutes — 100 total reps, no rest, no substitutions. One kettlebell. Three sessions per week. Three months. If you are new to kettlebell training and want to arrive somewhere real at the end of a program, this is the one that takes you there.

---

## The Philosophy Behind It

The hardstyle swing is the most important movement in kettlebell training. Not the most impressive — the most important. Every ballistic movement in the system is a variation of the hip hinge pattern that the swing establishes. The clean, the snatch, the clean and press — they are all built on the same foundation. Athletes who skip the swing and move directly to more complex movements eventually have to come back and rebuild what they missed. This program front-loads that work so you never have to revisit it.

The one-arm swing is chosen specifically over the two-hand swing because it trains the full-body tension and anti-rotation demand that the two-hand swing allows you to avoid. With one arm on the bell, the opposite side of your body has to work hard to stay square. That demand — resisting rotation under load — is the quality that makes the one-arm swing a genuine athletic movement and not just a conditioning drill. You learn this in Week 1 and reinforce it for 12 weeks.

The program is built in four phases, each with a distinct structural purpose.

Phase 1 (Weeks 1–3) trains the pattern with single-arm alternating sets — one arm per round, switching each interval — with generous rest and modest rep counts. The goal is not volume. It is the hip hinge. The bell should float at the top of each rep, which only happens when the hip snap is sharp and the body goes rigid at lockout. If the bell is being muscled up with the arm, the pattern is wrong, and the rest period exists to reset it.

Phase 2 (Weeks 4–6) introduces the paired format — both arms in one round, the format of the eventual test. Week 4 is the transition: a deload followed by the first session with 8 reps right, 8 reps left in a single interval. By Week 5 that becomes 9 per side, and by Week 5 Day 3 it becomes 10 per side — the exact rep count of The Standard. Week 6 builds volume at that rep count: 5 rounds, then 6, with rest compressing from 30 seconds to 20.

Phase 3 (Weeks 7–9) removes the rest. Not all at once — incrementally, over 9 sessions. Week 7 runs 30, 20, then 15 seconds of rest. Week 8 holds at 15 then drops to 10. Week 9 goes 10, 5, then zero. The first zero-rest session in Week 9 Day 3 is a preview, not a test — the block note says exactly that. You're finding out what 5 continuous minutes of swings feels like in your body before the program asks you to perform it.

Phase 4 (Weeks 10–11) cements the standard and then overreaches it. Week 10 runs the 5-round zero-rest standard, then adds a 6-round overreach session in the middle — 120 reps, no rest — before returning to the standard. The logic is simple: if 6 rounds feels manageable, 5 rounds on test day will feel like relief. Week 11 pushes further: 6 rounds, 5 rounds, then 7 rounds — 140 reps, no rest, peak volume of the entire program. After that, test week is a taper.

## Week by Week

**Week 1 — First Swings**

8 rounds of 5 reps per arm, alternating. 50 seconds of rest. The hike, the snap, the float — three things to find in every rep. Day 3 adds two rounds for the first volume accumulation of the program.

**Week 2 — Building Sets**

Rep targets increase to 6, then 7 by Day 3. Volume increases to 10 rounds. Rest tightens slightly. The first time 7 reps per arm feels easy is the moment the pattern has been earned.

**Week 3 — Volume Push**

12 rounds of 7 reps, then 10 rounds of 8, then 12 rounds of 8. The Phase 1 peak — 96 total reps in the final session of the week — is a meaningful number. The program acknowledges it: you've earned the next phase.

**Week 4 — Transition**

A deliberate deload followed by the introduction of the paired format on Day 3. 5 rounds, 8 reps right and 8 reps left in a single 50-second interval. This is where the training shifts from practice sets to test-format sets. The change in feel is immediate.

**Weeks 5–6 — Road to 10/10**

9 per side, then 10 per side. The full test format appears in Week 5 Day 3: 5 rounds of 10R + 10L with 30 seconds of rest. Week 6 builds volume at that standard and tightens rest to 20 seconds.

**Weeks 7–9 — Rest Compression**

Nine sessions. Nine rest reductions. 30 → 20 → 15 → 15 → 10 → 10 → 10 → 5 → 0. The first zero-rest session reveals exactly how much you've been relying on recovery. The pattern and the hip power have to carry you now.

**Weeks 10–11 — Cementing and Overreach**

The standard is trained repeatedly. 6-round and 7-round overreach sessions ensure that 5 rounds feels well within capacity. 140 reps in the final session of Week 11 is the most demanding single session of the program.

**Week 12 — Taper and Test**

A practice run, an active taper at reduced volume, then The Standard on Day 3. 100 reps. 5 minutes. No rest. The warm-up is calm. If the program has been followed faithfully, the test is not a surprise — it is a confirmation.

## What Success Looks Like

By the end of this program:

- You complete The Standard: 10 reps right, 10 reps left, every minute for 5 minutes, 100 total reps
- The bell floats at the top of every rep — you are not muscling it with your arm
- Anti-rotation is visible: your hips stay square and your core stays braced on every one-arm rep, not just the first few
- The overreach sessions in Weeks 10 and 11 (120 and 140 reps) felt hard but achievable — 100 reps on test day feels like a step back from the edge, not a leap to it
- Three sessions per week for 12 consecutive weeks — the habit of consistent training is as real an outcome as The Standard itself
