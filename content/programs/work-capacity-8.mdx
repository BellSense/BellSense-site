## What This Program Is

Work Capacity Protocol is an 8-week aerobic power development program built around long-duration continuous ballistic blocks. Four sessions per week, each anchored by 3-minute or longer work intervals across the snatch, swing, and clean. Where most kettlebell programs measure output in short bursts, this program asks you to sustain it over time — and to do so repeatedly, across a session, across a week, across 8 weeks. The benchmark is an 8-minute continuous snatch block on the final day. If sustained power output is the quality you're missing, this is the program that builds it.

---

## The Philosophy Behind It

Aerobic power — the ability to maintain meaningful force output over long durations — is the most underdeveloped quality in most kettlebell athletes. The training formats that dominate the space (EMOM, short intervals, heavy rest) are excellent for neural training and skill acquisition. They are not effective for developing the oxidative system, which is what determines how long you can sustain quality work before technical breakdown begins.

Work Capacity Protocol is built specifically to develop that quality. The training blocks are long — 3 minutes to 3.5 minutes of continuous work — with structured rest between them. The pacing strategy is conservative: the first session of any new format should feel manageable in the early rounds, not brutal. The aerobic system adapts through volume over time, not through intensity spikes. This is a consistent theme across all 8 weeks: steady pace, avoid early spikes, maintain power retention above 75%.

Each primary session in this program dedicates itself to one movement — a snatch day, a swing day, a clean day — and follows it with a secondary movement at shorter duration. The secondary movement serves as an aerobic bridge: additional volume at moderate intensity, linking the primary block to the recovery period while maintaining output. The Day 4 mixed session pairs a primary ballistic with a grind block for structural support — overhead press, row, or goblet squat — to ensure the strength base doesn't erode under the aerobic volume demand.

Progression moves through three phases. Weeks 1–2 establish the base: 3 blocks of 3-minute primary work. Weeks 3–4 add a round, bringing total blocks to 4. Weeks 5–6 extend the work intervals to 3.5 minutes and compress rest — the same round count, more total time under load, less buffer between rounds. Weeks 7–8 peak at 5 blocks with a benchmark on the final day.

The velocity metric the app tracks across these sessions tells a specific story: as aerobic capacity develops, the velocity spread within a block decreases. An athlete early in the program will spike output in the first minute, fade through the second, and grind through the third. An athlete who has adapted to this training will hold a steady pace across the full interval with minimal spread. That consistency — not a higher average velocity, but a flatter velocity curve — is the primary adaptation the program is developing.

## Week by Week

**Weeks 1–2 — Volume Introduction**

Four sessions per week, each with 3 primary blocks of 3-minute work at 90-second rest. Snatch day, swing day, clean day, and a mixed day that combines a primary ballistic with structural grind work. The instruction across all blocks is the same: steady pace, no early spike. If you go hard in minute one, you will pay for it in minute three. This is the pacing lesson the first two weeks are designed to teach. The goblet squat on the mixed day is 8 controlled reps — structural support, not performance.

**Weeks 3–4 — Volume Expansion**

A fourth primary block is added to each session. Same rest. Same work intervals. More total time under load. The target is velocity retention above 75% — the app will show you clearly whether the added round is degrading output across the block. The secondary movements extend slightly in duration. The mixed day swaps to the one-arm row for structural variety. By the end of Week 4, you've accumulated significant continuous ballistic volume across all three movements.

**Weeks 5–6 — Density Stress**

Work intervals extend to 3.5 minutes. Rest tightens to 75 seconds. Four primary blocks per session. This is where the aerobic adaptation from Weeks 1–4 is tested: can you sustain the pace you've been building with less recovery and longer intervals? The answer should be yes, but the margin is intentionally thinner. The mixed day adds the overhead press for structural support. The core activation in sessions shifts to the RKC Plank — active tension is prioritized as training demand peaks.

**Weeks 7–8 — Accumulation Peak and Benchmark**

Peak volume: 5 primary blocks per session at 3.5 minutes each. The secondary blocks reduce to 2 rounds — total session length is already at its maximum. Week 8 Day 4 is the benchmark: one continuous 8-minute snatch block. No rest. No switching protocol. Just 8 minutes of sustained snatch output. The warm-up is minimal. The cool-down is thorough. What you produce in those 8 minutes is the honest expression of what the program built.

## What Success Looks Like

By the end of this program:

- The 8-minute benchmark session is completed without a technique breakdown — snatch quality in minute 7 is recognizably the same as minute 1
- Velocity spread within each 3-minute block has narrowed across the program — your pace in minute 3 is closer to minute 1 than it was in Week 1
- Four training sessions per week has been sustained for 8 consecutive weeks — the frequency is embedded and the body is adapted to it
- The secondary movement blocks — the aerobic bridges after primary sessions — feel manageable rather than punishing, because aerobic recovery has genuinely improved
- Structural grind work on Day 4 has maintained consistent rep quality across all 8 weeks — the strength base held under aerobic volume demand
