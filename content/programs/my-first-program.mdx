## What This Program Is

My First Program is where your kettlebell training begins. Over four weeks, you'll learn three foundational ballistic movements — the swing, the clean, and the snatch — introduced one at a time, in the order they were designed to be learned. There is no conditioning pressure, no rep-count competition, and no ego involved. This program exists for one reason: to build the movement patterns you'll use for the rest of your training life.

---

## The Philosophy Behind It

Every session in this program is structured around an anti-glycolytic protocol. That means the rest periods are long — deliberately, intentionally long. You'll work for 20–30 seconds and rest for 75–110 seconds. That ratio will feel strange if you've ever done circuit training or HIIT. It should. This isn't that.

The goal of anti-glycolytic training for beginners isn't to condition you. It's to let you practice at full quality without accumulated fatigue distorting the pattern. When your rest is generous, each set feels like practice — because it is. You're not grinding through reps. You're printing movement into your nervous system.

The three movements are introduced in a deliberate sequence for a deliberate reason. The swing comes first because it establishes the hip hinge — the foundational pattern that drives every ballistic in kettlebell training. The clean comes second because it is a swing with a different destination: the bell ends up racked at the shoulder instead of floating out front. The snatch comes last because it is the most demanding — a swing that continues all the way overhead, requiring control at lockout and a safe descent. Attempting any of these out of order produces sloppy patterns that have to be unlearned later. The sequence exists to prevent that.

By the time you reach Week 4, you'll perform all three movements in a single session. That last session — the Grand Tour — is not a test. It is proof. Proof that the hinge pattern you built in Week 1 is the same pattern powering the swing, the clean, and the snatch. One movement, three expressions.

## Week by Week

**Week 1 — The Hip Hinge**

Three swing sessions. That's it. The hip hinge is the foundation, and this week you build it without anything else in the way. Sets run 20–25 seconds with over a minute of rest between them. The goal is not reps — it's pattern. Every rep should look and feel like your best rep. If it doesn't, the rest period gets longer, not shorter. You'll also begin core activation before each session and mobility work after: this becomes the structure of every session from here on.

**Week 2 — Adding the Clean**

The clean is introduced in the second session of this week, after a swing primer reinforces the hinge. The key teaching cue for the clean is the rack position: the bell settles into the shoulder — it doesn't crash into it. "Zip the jacket" is the image — elbow drives back, bell floats up, catch is quiet. Day 2 is clean-focused, giving you full attention on the catch without the swing preceding it. Day 3 puts both movements together for the first time. Same hinge, different destination.

**Week 3 — The Full Ballistic**

Week 3 introduces the snatch. The snatch is the highest-skill ballistic in kettlebell training — it demands a powerful hip snap, close-path mechanics, and a controlled overhead lockout. It arrives only after two weeks of hinge pattern development, because rushing it produces a muscled, ugly rep that puts the shoulder at risk. Extra rest is built into every snatch block. The third session of this week is the first time you'll train all three movements in one session — volume is reduced accordingly. Technique over totals.

**Week 4 — Putting It Together**

Volume increases across the board. The swing block on Day 1 reaches 10 rounds — the most work you've done in a single block. Day 2 pairs the clean and snatch without the swing. Day 3, the Grand Tour, runs swing, clean, and snatch back-to-back. The warmup cues you to notice how much easier the core activation is compared to Week 1. That's the program working. You started not knowing what a snatch was. You're finishing with all three movements in your hands.

## What Success Looks Like

By the end of this program:

- You can perform a two-hand swing with consistent hip snap and a float at the top — no muscling the bell, no squatting it up
- You can clean to a quiet rack position — bell settles, doesn't collide, forearm vertical
- You can snatch with a locked-out overhead position — arm straight, shoulder packed, controlled descent
- Your velocity retention across a swing block holds reasonably well round-to-round — you're not fading hard by round 4 out of 6
- The core activation and mobility blocks feel like part of training, not chores appended to it

If you can do all of the above, you're ready to train. This program gave you the movements. The next program builds on them.
