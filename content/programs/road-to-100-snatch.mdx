## What This Program Is

Road to 100 is a 12-week performance mesocycle with one goal: completing 100 kettlebell snatches as fast as possible. The hardstyle snatch test — 100 reps, traditionally in 5 minutes or less — is one of the oldest and most respected benchmarks in kettlebell sport. This program builds toward it across four structured phases: accumulation, density development, specificity, and a test week. If you've completed Foundations of Kettlebell Mastery and your snatch is clean and reliable, you're ready to start.

---

## The Philosophy Behind It

Most athletes approach a volume goal like 100 snatches by practicing 100 snatches — or something close to it — from the beginning. This produces a specific kind of failure: they get comfortable with mediocre reps under fatigue, train poor mechanics into their nervous system, and plateau well short of the goal. Road to 100 takes the opposite approach.

The first four weeks deliberately avoid long snatch sets. Instead, they build snatch volume through EMOM intervals — Every Minute On the Minute, 10 reps per arm — with strict rest between rounds. This format demands that every rep is a quality rep, because the pace is controlled and the rest is provided. The EMOM rounds increase from 10 to 16 over the accumulation phase, building the volume base without compromising the pattern.

Structural strength work is integrated throughout the entire program — overhead press, goblet squat, one-arm row — because a sustainable snatch is built on structural integrity. The press builds the shoulder and scapular strength to receive the bell overhead without compensation. The squat maintains posterior chain depth. The row balances the pulling pattern. These are not supplementary — they are maintenance work that keeps the body capable of 12 weeks of snatch volume. Their load reduces in the specificity phase as snatch priority increases, but they never disappear entirely.

The Turkish get-up appears every week in the recovery sessions. It is the single best diagnostic tool in kettlebell training: if the get-up quality degrades mid-program, something is accumulating that hasn't been addressed. It's also active recovery — slow, deliberate movement on a day when the nervous system needs output without intensity.

Progression is tracked by velocity retention. The app measures your first-third versus last-third average velocity on every snatch block. If you're retaining above 65%, the session advances. If you fall below 35%, the session repeats. This matters for the snatch specifically because the test is a race against both time and technique — velocity dropping is the leading indicator that form is breaking down before the reps do.

## Week by Week

**Weeks 1–4 — Accumulation**

Four sessions per week: an EMOM volume day, a structural strength day, a snatch ladder day, and a recovery day. The EMOM starts at 10 rounds of 10 reps per arm and builds to 16 rounds over the phase. Ladder days run ascending rep schemes — 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 reps per side — which train the ability to accelerate into higher rep counts rather than pacing from the start. Structural strength work is at full load. Recovery days are get-ups and light swings. By the end of Week 4, you've built a snatch volume base without ever doing a continuous set.

**Weeks 5–8 — Density Development**

The format shifts. EMOM intervals give way to 3-minute density blocks where the goal is maximum quality reps with alternating arms. Broken 100s appear in Week 5: four rounds of 25 snatches with 60 seconds of rest between rounds — 100 total reps, interrupted. Rest compresses over the four weeks. By Week 8, the 25-rep rounds are done with 30 seconds of rest between them. The structural strength work reduces in total reps but stays. Recovery days continue with get-ups. By the end of this phase, you've handled 100 snatches in a session — broken across sets, not yet continuously.

**Weeks 9–11 — Specificity**

The program now trains specifically for the test. Days 1 of these weeks are time exposure sessions: 75 reps for time in Week 9, 85 in Week 10, 90 in Week 11 — all within a 5-minute window. These sessions teach pacing strategy and expose the exact point where the rep rate begins to break down. Day 3 simulates the broken 100 in progressively longer unbroken chunks — 40/30/30 in Week 9, building to 60/20/20 in Week 11. Strength work drops to maintenance reps. The nervous system is prioritizing one thing now.

**Week 12 — Taper and Test**

Two light sessions — snatch at easy pace, get-ups, minimal volume — then the test on Day 3. 100 snatches. For time. The sub-5:00 goal is real and achievable if the program has been run faithfully. The warm-up is deliberate and calm. The cool-down afterward is long. You've earned it.

## What Success Looks Like

By the end of this program:

- You complete 100 snatches in a single session — no program for you is complete without this
- Your time is under 5 minutes, or meaningfully close if this is your first attempt
- Velocity retention across the time exposure sessions in Weeks 9–11 holds above 65% — your last reps are recognizably the same movement as your first
- The structural strength work — press, squat, row — has remained consistent at the prescribed rep targets through Weeks 1–8 without degradation
- The get-up sessions in recovery weeks feel easier in Week 12 than they did in Week 1 — the stability base has grown
